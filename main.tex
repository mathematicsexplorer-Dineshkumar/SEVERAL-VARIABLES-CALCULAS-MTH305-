{\LARGE \textbf{\textit{Summary of lectures}}}

\vspace{1cm}

The fundamental idea of calculus is ``local approximation'' of functions by linear functions. This idea is completely obscured in the traditional treatment of single variable calculus, where the derivative of a function at a point is defined as a real number obtained as the limit of the difference quotient. Not only does this fail to reflect the true essence of the concept of the derivative of a function at a point, also the expression $\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}$ where $f$ is a real valued function defined on an open interval in R containing the point $a$, does not extend to functions of several variables.

\vspace{1cm}

Since the concept of a derivative must be intrinsic, a general framework is necessary. Henceforth, we adopt a ``geometric'' perspective on analysis, aiming to remain as close as possible to the fundamental idea of calculus.


\begin{center}
{\LARGE \textbf{\textit{*****}}}
\end{center}
Let $K := \mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$, and $E$ and $F$ are Banach spaces over $K$. Suppose that $U \subseteq E$ is an open subset of $E$ and $f, g : U \to F$. We say that $f$ and $g$ are \textit{tangential to each other at $a \in U$}, or simply \textit{tangent at $a$}, if

\[
\lim_{x \to a} \frac{\|f(x) - g(x)\|}{\|x - a\|} = 0.
\]

\textit{\textbf{Note:}} Being tangential to each other at a given point remains unaffected if equivalent norms replace one or both of the norms.

\vspace{1cm}

\textbf{Definition 1:} A function $f : U \to F$ is said to be differentiable at $a \in U$ if there exists a continuous linear map $A : E \to F$ such that the function $x \mapsto f(a) + A(x - a)$ is tangential to $f$ at $a$, i.e.,

\[
\lim_{x \to a} \frac{\|f(x) - f(a) - A(x - a)\|}{\|x - a\|} = 0.
\]

The above is equivalent to saying that $f(a + h) - f(a) = Ah + o(\|h\|)$. In simpler terms, $f(a + h) - f(a)$ can be divided into two parts: the first part is linear in $h$, while the second part approaches 0 faster than $h$ as $h$ approaches 0.

If $f$ is differentiable at $a$, then the continuous linear map $A$ mentioned in the above definition is unique. This can easily be proved using the following exercise:

\vspace{1cm}

\textbf{Exercise 1:}

Show that if $T \in L(E; F)$ satisfies $\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\|Tx\|}{\|x\|} = 0$, then $T = 0$.

That unique continuous linear map is called the derivative of $f$ at $a$, and denoted by $f'(a)$ or $Df(a)$ or $(df)_a$. Thus we obtain that $f'(a)$ is the unique continuous linear map from $E$ to $F$ satisfying

\[
f(a + h) - f(a) = f'(a)h + o(\|h\|).
\]

\vspace{1cm}
\textbf{Remark 1:} Suppose that $E = K$, and $U \subseteq_{open} K$. If $f : U \to F$ is differentiable at $a \in U$, then $f'(a)$ is a continuous linear map from the one dimensional $K$ vector space $K$ to $F$. Hence, one obtains that

\[
\lim_{h \to 0} \left\| \frac{f(a + h) - f(a)}{h} - f'(a)(1) \right\|
=
\lim_{h \to 0} \left\| \frac{f(a + h) - f(a)}{|h|} - f'(a)h \right\|
= 0,
\]

i.e., $\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h} = f'(a)(1)$. Conversely, if $\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}$ exists in $F$, say $\ell$, then

\[
\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\|f(a + h) - f(a) - h\ell\|}{|h|} = 0,
\]

which shows that $f$ is differentiable at $a$, and moreover, $f'(a)h = h\ell$, for all $h \in K$. 

Thus we see that, in the special case $E = K$, the differentiability of $f : U \to F$ at $a \in U$ is equivalent to the existence of the limit 

$$\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}$$.

Further, in view of the isomorphism $L(K; F) \to F$, $A \mapsto A(1)$, which is also an isometry, the derivative $f'(a)$ is naturally identified with 

$$\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h} \in F$$. 

In particular, if $F = K$, then $f'(a)$ can be regarded as the number defined by $\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}$. This justifies the numerical interpretation of the derivative of a function at a given point in the one dimensional scenario.

Suppose that $f : U \to F$ is differentiable at $a \in U$, where $U \subseteq_{open} E$. Then there exists $\eta > 0$ such that 



$\|f(a + h) - f(a) - f'(a)h\| \leq \|h\|$, for all $h \in B(0; \eta)$. It now follows that
\[
\|f(a + h) - f(a)\|
\leq \|f(a + h) - f(a) - f'(a)h\| + \|f'(a)h\|
\leq \|h\| + \|f'(a)\|\|h\|
= (1 + \|f'(a)\|)\|h\|, \quad \forall h \in B(0; \eta).
\]
As a consequence of this, we obtain that  \textbf{\textit{$f$ is continuous at $a$.}}


\textbf{Example 1:} Let $f : U \to F$ be a constant map. Then it is easy to see that $f$ is differentiable at each point of $U$ and $f'(a) = 0$, for all $a \in U$.

\textbf{Example 2:} Let $A \in L(E; F)$ and $f : U \to F$, $f(x) := Ax$. Then, for all $a \in U$, $f$ is differentiable at $a$, and $f'(a) = A$.

\textbf{Example 3:} Let $E_1, E_2$ and $F$ be Banach spaces over $K$. Recall that the following operations make $E_1 \times E_2$ into a vector space over $K$:

\[
(x_1, x_2) + (y_1, y_2) := (x_1 + y_1, x_2 + y_2); \quad \text{and} \quad \alpha(x_1, x_2) := (\alpha x_1, \alpha x_2), \ \forall x_1, y_1 \in E_1, x_2, y_2 \in E_2, \alpha \in K.
\]

Further, the following defines a norm in $E_1 \times E_2$:

\[
\|(x_1, x_2)\| = \max\{\|x_1\|, \|x_2\|\}, \ \text{for all } (x_1, x_2) \in E_1 \times E_2.
\]

Since the spaces $E_1$ and $E_2$ are complete with respect to their respective norms, it follows that the product space $E_1 \times E_2$ is a Banach space under the norm defined above. Consider a continuous bilinear map $f : E_1 \times E_2 \to F$. We show that the restriction of $f$ to any open subset of $U$ of $E_1 \times E_2$ is differentiable at each $(a, b) \in U$. To see this, observe that

\[
f(a + h, b + k) = f(a, b) + f(h, b) + f(a, k) + f(h, k), \ \text{whenever } \|(h, k)\| \text{ is sufficiently small.}
\]

It is easy to see that the map

\[
E_1 \times E_2 \to F, \quad (h, k) \mapsto f(h, b) + f(a, k)
\]

is continuous and linear. Now the differentiability of $f$ at $(a, b)$ is immediate in view of the following:

\[
0 \leq \lim_{(h,k) \to (0,0)} \frac{\|f(h, k)\|}{\|(h, k)\|}
\leq \lim_{(h,k) \to (0,0)} \frac{\|f\|\|h\|\|k\|}{\|(h, k)\|}
\leq \lim_{(h,k) \to (0,0)} \frac{\|f\|\|(h, k)\|^2}{\|(h, k)\|}
= \lim_{(h,k) \to (0,0)} \|f\|\|(h, k)\| = 0.
\]

Note that, in this case, for all $(a, b) \in U$, we have

\[
f'(a, b)(h, k) = f(h, b) + f(a, k), \ \forall (h, k) \in E_1 \times E_2.
\]

\textbf{Exercise 2:}

Generalize the above conclusion for multilinear maps $f : E_1 \times \cdots \times E_n \to F$, where $n \in \mathbb{N}$ and $E_1, \ldots, E_n$ are Banach spaces over $K$.

As a straightforward application of Exercise 2, we obtain that the determinant map defined as follows is differentiable at any $A \in M_n(K)$:

\[
\det : M_n(K) \cong \underbrace{K^n \times \cdots \times K^n}_{n \text{ times}} \to K, \quad A = (A_1, \ldots, A_n) \mapsto \det A,
\]

where $A_i$ stands for the $i$-th column of $A$, and further, we have $\det'(A)H = \operatorname{tr}((\operatorname{adj} A)H)$, for all $H \in M_n(K)$.
\newpage
\textbf{Example 4 :} Let $A$ be a Banach algebra over $K$. For any $n \in \mathbb{N}$, consider the map $f : A \to A$, $f(x) := x^n$. Since

\[
(x + h)^n - x^n = \sum_{k=1}^{n} x^{k-1} h x^{n-k} + o(\|h\|),
\]

$f$ is differentiable everywhere, and for all $x \in A$, we have $f'(x)h := \sum_{k=1}^{n} x^{k-1} h x^{n-k}, \ \forall h \in A$.

\vspace{1cm}
\textbf{Example 5 :} Recall that, for any $n \in \mathbb{N}$, $GL_n(K)$ is an open subset of $M_n(K)$, since it is the inverse image of $K \setminus \{0\}$ under the determinant function, which is continuous. Consider the map $f : GL_n(K) \to GL_n(K)$, $f(A) := A^{-1}$. Note that the entries of $A^{-1}$ are rational functions in $a_{ij}$'s, where $A = (a_{ij})_{1 \leq i,j \leq n}$, since $A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det A} \cdot \operatorname{adj} A$. Consequently, $f$ is a continuous map. For any $H \in M_n(K)$, one has

\[
\|(A + H)^{-1} - A^{-1} - (-A^{-1} H A^{-1})\|
=
\|((A + H)^{-1} - A^{-1}) H A^{-1}\|
\leq
\|(A + H)^{-1} - A^{-1}\| \|H\| \|A^{-1}\|.
\]

From this, in view of the continuity of $f$ at $A$, it follows that $f$ is differentiable at $A$, and further one has $f'(A)(H) := -A^{-1} H A^{-1}$, for all $H \in M_n(K)$.

\vspace{1cm}
\textbf{Remark 2:} The above example can be generalised for isomorphisms between arbitrary Banach spaces $E$ and $F$ over $K$. Indeed, the set of all isomorphisms from $E$ to $F$, denoted by 
$$\operatorname{Isom}(E; F)$$
is an open subset of $L(E; F)$ and the map

$\operatorname{Isom}(E; F) \to \operatorname{Isom}(F; E)$, $A \mapsto A^{-1}$ is continuous. 

Then a verbatim repetition of the argument in Example 5 establishes differentiability. Similar reasoning can also be adopted to show that in any Banach algebra $A$, the set of all units, denoted by $A^\times$, is open in $A$, 

and the map $A^\times \to A^\times$, $a \mapsto a^{-1}$ is differentiable. See Exercise Sheet 1 for details. 

In particular, when $E = F = K$, $\operatorname{Isom}(K; K) \cong K \setminus \{0\}$, and one deduces the familiar formula
\[
\frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{1}{x}\right) = -\frac{1}{x^2}, \ \text{for all } x \in K \setminus \{0\}.
\]

\textbf{Example 6 :} Let $A$ be a Banach algebra over $K$. For any fixed $x \in A$, consider the one variable function $f : K \to A$, $f(t) := e^{tx}$. Since, for any $t \in K$,

\[
\frac{f(t + h) - f(t)}{h} - xe^{tx}
=
\left( \frac{e^{hx} - 1 - hx}{h} \right) e^{tx}
=
\left( \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{h^{\,n-1} x^n}{n!} \right) e^{tx}
\longrightarrow 0, \quad h \to 0,
\]

one concludes that $f$ is differentiable at every $t \in K$, and identifies $f'(t)$ with the element $xe^{tx} \in F$ (see Remark 1). Alternatively, as $e^{tx} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{t^n x^n}{n!}$ is a power series in one variable with coefficients in $A$, we can justify the term-by-term differentiation (See Exercise Sheet 1 for details) which also leads to the same conclusion.

We now turn to the formal rules of derivation. To this end, first we prove the Chain rule.

\newpage
\begin{center}
{\LARGE \textbf{\textit{CHAIN RULE}}}
\end{center}

\textbf{Chain rule:} Let $E, F$ and $G$ be Banach spaces over $K$, $U$ and $V$ be open subsets of $E$ and $F$ respectively, and $U \xrightarrow{f} V \xrightarrow{g} G$. Assume that $f$ is differentiable at $a \in U$ and $g$ is differentiable at $f(a)$. Then $g \circ f$ is differentiable, and $(g \circ f)'(a) = g'(f(a)) \circ f'(a)$.

Thus, we see that the derivative of the composition is just the composition of the individual derivatives. For a detailed proof, please refer to the class notes.

An immediate application of the Chain rule is as follows. Suppose that $F_1, \ldots, F_m$ are Banach spaces over $K$, and 
$$f : U \to F_1 \times \cdots \times F_m$$ 
where $U$ is an open subset of a Banach space $E$ over $K$. For each $i = 1, \ldots, m$, denote by $p_i$ the projection map 
$F_1 \times \cdots \times F_m \to F_i$, $(y_1, \ldots, y_m) \mapsto y_i$
onto the $i$th factor, and by $f_i$ the $i$th coordinate of the map $f$, i.e., $f_i := p_i \circ f$. Since each $p_i$ is continuous and linear, if $f$ is differentiable at $a$, then $f_i$ is differentiable at $a$, for all $i = 1, \ldots, m$. To see how $f'(a)$ is related to the $f_i'(a)$'s, consider the canonical inclusion map defined as follows:
\[
u_i : F_i \to F_1 \times \cdots \times F_m, \quad
u_i(x) := (0, \ldots, 0, x, 0, \ldots, 0),
\]
for each $i = 1, \ldots, m$. Then $\sum_{i=1}^{m} u_i \circ p_i$ becomes the identity map on $F_1 \times \cdots \times F_m$, whence $\sum_{i=1}^{m} u_i \circ f_i = f$. Note that all $u_i$'s are continuous and linear. Hence, one obtains that

\[
f'(a) = \sum_{i=1}^{m} u_i \circ f_i'(a),
\tag{1}
\]

which simply means that $f'(a) = (f_1'(a), \ldots, f_m'(a))$.

\textbf{\textit{The Converse}} of the above is not difficult to see. Given that each $f_i$ is differentiable at $a$, one considers the following map

\[
T : E \to F_1 \times \cdots \times F_m, \; h \mapsto (f_1'(a)h, \ldots, f_m'(a)h).
\]

Clearly, $T$ is continuous and linear. Then from
\[
\|f(a + h) - f(a) - Th\|
=
\|(f_1(a + h) - f_1(a) - f_1'(a)h), \ldots, (f_m(a + h) - f_m(a) - f_m'(a)h)\|
=
\sup_{1 \leq i \leq m} \|f_i(a + h) - f_i(a) - f_i'(a)h\|,
\]
the differentiability of $f$ at $a$, and the relation $f'(a) = (f_1'(a), \ldots, f_m'(a))$ is clear.

\subsection*{***}\textbf{\textit{Suppose}} that $U$ is an open subset of a Banach space $E$ over $K$ and $f$ and $g$ are functions defined on $U$ taking values in the Banach spaces $F_1$ and $F_2$ over $K$ respectively. If $f$ and $g$ both are differentiable at $a \in U$, then from the above, we get that the map 

$U \to F_1 \times F_2$, $x \mapsto (f(x), g(x))$ is differentiable at $a$. 

Let $\varphi : F_1 \times F_2 \to G$ be a continuous linear or bilinear map, 
where $G$ is a Banach space over $K$. Then the Chain rule yields that the composite map $x \mapsto \varphi(f(x), g(x))$ is differentiable at $a$. Furthermore, its derivative at $a$ is readily seen to be $\varphi \circ (f'(a), g'(a))$ or the map $E \to G$, $h \mapsto \varphi(f'(a)h, g(a)) + \varphi(f(a), g'(a)h)$ accordingly as $\varphi$ is linear or bilinear respectively. We consider the following special cases of this:

1. If $F_1 = F_2 = F$, say, and $\varphi : F \times F \to F$, $\varphi(\xi, \zeta) := \xi + \zeta$, then $(f + g)'(a) = f'(a) + g'(a)$.

2. If $F_1 = F_2 = K$ and $\varphi : K \times K \to K$, $\varphi(\xi, \zeta) := \xi \zeta$, then $(fg)'(a) = g(a)f'(a) + f(a)g'(a)$.

3. If $E = K = F_1$, $F_2 = E$ and $\varphi : K \times E \to E$, $\varphi(\alpha, \xi) := \alpha \xi$, then $(fg)'(a) = f'(a)g(a) + f(a)g'(a)$.

\textbf{Remark 3:} If $E = K$ then the derivative considered above is identified with $\varphi(f'(a), g'(a))$ or $\varphi(f'(a), g(a)) + \varphi(f(a), g'(a))$, accordingly as $\varphi$ is linear or bilinear respectively.

Let $E$ and $F$ be Banach spaces over $K$, $a \in U \subseteq_{open} E$ and $f : U \to F$. Suppose that $t_0 \in I \subseteq_{open} K$ and $\gamma : I \to U$ is such that $\gamma(t_0) = a$ and $\gamma$ is differentiable at $t_0$. Then

\[
f \text{ is differentiable at } a \implies (f \circ \gamma)'(t_0) = f'(a)\gamma'(t_0).
\]

Given any $v \in E$, one considers $\gamma_{a,v}(t) := a + tv$, for all $t \in K$. Using the continuity of $\gamma_{a,v}$ at $0$, one obtains $\varepsilon > 0$ such that $\gamma_{a,v}(t) \in U$, whenever $|t| < \varepsilon$. The derivative $(f \circ \gamma_{a,v})'(0) = \frac{d}{dt}\big|_{t=0} f(a + tv)$, provided it exists, is called the directional derivative of $f$ at $a$ along $v$. It now follows from the above that, if $f$ is differentiable at $a$, then for all $v \in E$, the directional derivative $\frac{d}{dt}\big|_{t=0} f(a + tv)$ exists and equals to $f'(a)v$. The converse is false. In fact, having all directional derivatives at a given point does not suffice to ensure the continuity at that point. See Exercise Sheet 1.

Given Banach spaces $E_1, \ldots, E_n$ spaces over $K$ and $a = (a_1, \ldots, a_n) \in E_1 \times \cdots \times E_n$, consider the following map
\[
\lambda_j(x) := (a_1, \ldots, a_{j-1}, x, a_{j+1}, \ldots, a_n), \quad \forall x \in E_j,
\]
for each $j = 1, \ldots, n$. This is obviously continuous. If $U \subseteq_{open} E := E_1 \times \cdots \times E_n$ and $a \in U$, then for each $j = 1, \ldots, n$, from the continuity of $\lambda_j$ at $a_j$, one has an open neighborhood, say $N(a_j)$, of $a_j$ such that $\lambda_j(N(a_j)) \subseteq U$. Let $f : U \to F$. Clearly the composition $N(a_j) \xrightarrow{\lambda_j} U \xrightarrow{f} F$ makes sense for all $j = 1, \ldots, n$. For any $j = 1, \ldots, n$, the map $f \circ \lambda_j$ is called the $j$th partial map, and if it is differentiable at $a_j$, the derivative $(f \circ \lambda_j)'(a_j) \in L(E_j; F)$ is referred to as the $j$th partial derivative of $f$ at $a$ and it is denoted by $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_j}(a)$ or simply by $\partial_j f(a)$.

\textbf{\textit{Note:}} If $E_1 = \cdots = E_n = K$, then $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_j}(a)$ is same as the directional derivative of $f$ at $a$ along $e_j := (0, \ldots, 0, 1, 0, \ldots, 0)$.

Observe that, each $\lambda_j$ is differentiable at $a_j$, and $\lambda_j'(a_j) = v_j$, where $v_j : E_j \to E$ is the canonical inclusion $x \mapsto (0, \ldots, 0, x, 0, \ldots, 0)$. Now if $f$ is assumed to be differentiable at $a$, then it follows from the Chain rule that $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_j}(a)$ exists and $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_j}(a) = f'(a) \circ v_j$. For all $j = 1, \ldots, n$, consider the projection map $q_j : E_1 \times \cdots \times E_n \to E_j$ onto the $j$th factor. Then from the relation $\sum_{j=1}^{n} v_j \circ q_j = I_E$, we get the following:
\[
f'(a)
=
\sum_{j=1}^{n} f'(a) \circ (v_j \circ q_j)
=
\sum_{j=1}^{n} (f'(a) \circ v_j) \circ q_j
=
\sum_{j=1}^{n} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_j}(a) \circ q_j,
\tag{2}
\]
i.e., for all $h := (h_1, \ldots, h_n) \in E_1 \times \cdots \times E_n$, $f'(a)h = \sum_{j=1}^{n} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_j}(a) h_j$.

\textbf{Exercise 7:}

Let $E$ and $F$ be Banach spaces over $K$, $U \subseteq_{open} E$, and $f : U \to F$. If $f$ is differentiable on $U$, then the derivative of $f$, denoted by $f'$, is defined by
\[
f' : U \to L(E; F), \quad x \mapsto f'(x).
\]
If $f'$ is continuous, then we say that $f$ is of class $C^1$, and denote this by $f \in C^1(U)$, or simply by $f \in C^1$, provided there is no danger of confusion. Show that, if $E = E_1 \times \cdots \times E_n$, then for any differentiable map $f : U \to F$,
\[
f \in C^1(U) \Longleftrightarrow \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_j} : U \to L(E_j; F) \text{ is continuous for all } j = 1, \ldots, n.
\]
Further, if $F = F_1 \times \cdots \times F_m$, where $F_1, \ldots, F_m$ are Banach spaces over $K$, combining (1) and (2), we arrive at
\[
f'(a)
=
\sum_{i=1}^{m} u_i \circ f_i'(a)
=
\sum_{i=1}^{m} \sum_{j=1}^{n} u_i \circ \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}(a) \circ q_j.
\tag{3}
\]
Thus from (3), we see that $f'(a)$ is determined by the $mn$ continuous linear maps $\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}(a) \in L(E_j; F_i)$, where $i = 1, \ldots, m$ and $j = 1, \ldots, n$.

\textbf{Exercise 8:}

\]

\[
\begin{pmatrix}
\frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_1}(a) & \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_2}(a) & \cdots & \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_n}(a) \\
\frac{\partial f_2}{\partial x_1}(a) & \frac{\partial f_2}{\partial x_2}(a) & \cdots & \frac{\partial f_2}{\partial x_n}(a) \\

\end{pmatrix}.

\[
f'(a)e_\ell
=
\sum_{i=1}^{m} u_i \left( \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_\ell}(a) \right)
=
\left( \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_\ell}(a), \ldots, \frac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_\ell}(a) \right),

\textbf{Example 7:}

I. Let $E$ be a Banach space over $K$ and $N$ be any norm in $E$. It is easy to see that the directional derivative of $N$ at the origin along any nonzero vector does not exist. Consequently, $N$ is not differentiable at the origin.

II. For $n \in \mathbb{N}$, consider the euclidean norm $\|\cdot\|_2$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$, i.e., $\|x\|_2 := \sqrt{x_1^2 + \cdots + x_n^2}$, for all $x = (x_1, \ldots, x_n) \in \mathbb{R}^n$. Then, from the above and the chain rule, it follows that $\|\cdot\|_2$ is differentiable everywhere except at the origin.

III. Consider $\|\cdot\|_1$ norm in $\mathbb{R}^n$, i.e., $\|x\|_1 := |x_1| + \cdots + |x_n|$, for all $x = (x_1, \ldots, x_n) \in \mathbb{R}^n$. Then $\|\cdot\|_1$ is differentiable everywhere except the coordinate axes.

Then $\|\cdot\|_\infty$ is differentiable precisely at all points $(x_1, \ldots, x_n) \in \mathbb{R}^n$ such that there exists some $i \in \{1, \ldots, n\}$ with the property that $|x_i| > |x_j|$ for all $j \neq i$. \textbf{Rademacher’s theorem:}

\textbf{\textit{Let $n, m \in \mathbb{N}$, $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$, and $f : U \to \mathbb{R}^m$ be Lipschitz. Then $f$ is differentiable almost everywhere with respect to the Lebesgue measure on $\mathbb{R}^n$.}}

Recall that every complex vector space can also be regarded as a real vector space. If $V$ and $W$ are complex vector spaces and $T : V \to W$ is linear, then $T$ is also linear when $V$ and $W$ are regarded as real vector spaces. The converse of this is not true. The following is a simple example:

\[
T : \mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{C}, \; T(z) := \overline{z}.
\tag{5}
\]

This is obviously not linear when $\mathbb{C}$ is considered as a complex vector space, but linear when we consider $\mathbb{C}$ is viewed as a vector space over $\mathbb{R}$. To distinguish between maps that are linear when $V$ and $W$ are considered as vector spaces over $\mathbb{C}$ from those that are linear when $V$ and $W$ are regarded as real vector spaces, we adopt the following convention. If $T : V \to W$ is linear when $V$ and $W$ are considered as complex vector spaces, we say that $T$ is $\mathbb{C}$-linear, while if $T$ is linear when $V$ and $W$ are considered as real vector spaces, $T$ is said to be $\mathbb{R}$-linear. As mentioned above, every $\mathbb{C}$-linear map is trivially $\mathbb{R}$-linear.

It is obvious that a norm in a complex vector space $V$ would also be a norm when $V$ is considered as a vector space over $\mathbb{R}$. Hence, it yields the same metric on the underlying set $V$ regardless of whether it is considered as a real or complex vector space. From this, it follows that, for any two complex vector spaces $V$ and $W$ and $T \in L(V; W)$, $T$ is also continuous $\mathbb{R}$-linear.

Suppose that $E$ and $F$ are complex Banach spaces, $U \subseteq E$, and $f : U \to F$ is differentiable at $a \in U$. Now if $E$ and $F$ are viewed as real Banach spaces, the differentiability of $f$ at $a$ remains unaffected. Indeed, the original continuous $\mathbb{C}$-linear map $f'(a)$, when considered as a continuous $\mathbb{R}$-linear map, becomes the derivative of $f$ at $a$ in this case. 

It is customary to say $f$ is $\mathbb{C}$-differentiable at $a$ in the former, and $f$ is $\mathbb{R}$-differentiable at $a$ in the latter. Thus, if $f$ is $\mathbb{C}$-differentiable at $a$, then it must be $\mathbb{R}$-differentiable at $a$. However, $\mathbb{R}$-differentiability at a point $a \in U$ does not imply the $\mathbb{C}$-differentiability at $a$. 

The map mentioned above in (5) is clearly differentiable at the origin when $\mathbb{C}$ is treated as a real vector space. 

However, when one considers $\mathbb{C}$ as a vector space over $\mathbb{C}$, $T$ is not $\mathbb{C}$-differentiable at the origin, since $\lim_{z \to 0} \frac{\overline{z}}{z}$ does not exist. 

A moment’s thought would reflect that if $f$ is $\mathbb{R}$-differentiable at $a \in U$, then it is $\mathbb{C}$-differentiable if and only if the continuous $\mathbb{R}$-linear map $f'(a) : E \to F$ is $\mathbb{C}$-linear, since the derivative is unique. 

\textbf{\textit{We now find a necessary and sufficient condition for that in the special case $\dim_{\mathbb{C}} E = \dim_{\mathbb{C}} F = 1$.}}

Let $T : \mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{C}$ be an $\mathbb{R}$-linear map. It is easy to see that $T$ is $\mathbb{C}$-linear if and only if

\[
T(i(x + iy)) = iT(x + iy), \ \forall x + iy \in \mathbb{C}.
\tag{6}
\]

Consider the matrix representation
\[
\begin{pmatrix}
a & c \\
b & d
\end{pmatrix}
\]
of $T$ relative to the ordered basis $\{1, i\}$ of the real vector space $\mathbb{C}$.

\textit{Note:} In view of the identification $\mathbb{C} \leftrightarrow \mathbb{R}^2$, $x + iy \leftrightarrow (x, y)$, the above matrix is the matrix representation of $T$ relative to the standard basis of $\mathbb{R}^2$ when we regard $T$ as a linear map on the real vector space $\mathbb{R}^2$.

It follows that (6) is equivalent to the following:

\[
\begin{pmatrix}
a & c \\
b & d
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
-y \\
x
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
-bx - dy \\
ax + cy
\end{pmatrix},
\quad \forall (x, y) \in \mathbb{R}^2.
\tag{7}
\]

One can easily verify that (7) holds when and only when $a = d$ and $b = -c$. Thus, we prove that an $\mathbb{R}$-linear map on $\mathbb{C}$ is $\mathbb{C}$-linear if and only if its matrix representation relative to the ordered basis $\{1, i\}$ of vector space $\mathbb{C}$ over $\mathbb{R}$ is of the form

\[
\begin{pmatrix}
a & -b \\
b & a
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

\end{pmatrix}.
\]

Hence, we conclude that $f$ is holomorphic at $a$ if and only if $\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}(a) = \frac{\partial v}{\partial y}(a)$ and $\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}(a) = -\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}(a)$, i.e., $f$ satisfies the Cauchy-Riemann equations at $a$.

Let $a < b$ and $f : [a, b) \to F$, where $F$ is a Banach space over $\mathbb{R}$. We say that $f$ has a right hand derivative at a point $x \in [a, b)$ if $\lim_{h \to 0+} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$ exists, and in that case the limit is referred to as the right hand derivative of $f$ at $x$ and denoted by $f'(x+)$.

    \textbf{\textit{Mean Value Theorem}}
\end{center}

\textbf{Mean value theorem:}
Let $F$ be a Banach space over $\mathbb{R}$. Suppose that $f : [a, b] \to F$ and $g : [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuous maps defined on the compact interval $[a, b] \subseteq \mathbb{R}$. Assume that there exists a countable subset $D$ of $[a, b]$ such that for all $x \in (a, b) \setminus D$, $f'(x+)$ and $g'(x+)$ exist and $\|f'(x+)\| \leq g'(x+)$. Then $\|f(b) - f(a)\| \leq g(b) - g(a)$.

\textbf{Remark 5:} The countability assumption on $D$ in the Mean value theorem cannot be replaced by the weaker assumption that $D$ is Lebesgue null. The Cantor-Lebesgue function is an increasing, surjective self-map on the interval $[0, 1]$, which has a zero derivative Lebesgue a.e.

\textbf{Corollary 1:}

I. Let $g : [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ be a continuous function defined on the compact interval $[a, b] \subseteq \mathbb{R}$. Assume that $g'(x+)$ exists and nonnegative for all but countably many $x \in (a, b)$. Then $g$ is increasing on $[a, b]$.

II. Let $F$ be a Banach space over $\mathbb{R}$ and $f : [a, b] \to F$ be continuous. If there exists $C > 0$ such that $f'(x+)$ exists and satisfies $\|f'(x+)\| \leq C$, for all but countably many $x \in (a, b)$, then $f$ is Lipschitz on $[a, b]$ with Lipschitz constant $C$. In particular, if $f$ has a zero right hand derivative at all but countably many points in the open interval $(a, b)$, then $f$ must be a constant function.

\textbf{Corollary 2:} Let $E$ and $F$ be Banach spaces over $\mathbb{R}$, $U \subseteq_{open} E$, and $f : U \to F$ be differentiable. If $f' \equiv 0$, then $f$ is locally constant. Moreover, if $U$ is connected, then $f$ is constant.

This example shows that the familiar equality in the Mean value theorem in calculus of one real variable does not hold for functions in several variables; and, on the other hand, "$<$" can occur in (8).

\section*{\textbf{Differentiability under uniform convergence:}}
Let $U$ be an open connected subset of a Banach space $E$ over $\mathbb{R}$, and for all $n \in \mathbb{N}$, $f_n : U \to F$ be differentiable, where $F$ is a real Banach space. Suppose that the sequence $\{f_n'\}_{n=1}^{\infty}$ of maps from $U$ to $L(E; F)$ converges to $g : U \to L(E; F)$ locally uniformly, i.e., for every $x \in U$, there exists $r_x > 0$ such that $f_n' \xrightarrow[n \to \infty]{} g$ uniformly on $B(x; r_x)$. Assume that, the sequence $\{f_n(a)\}_{n=1}^{\infty}$ is convergent for some $a \in U$. Then there exists a function $f : U \to F$ satisfying the following:

I. $f_n \xrightarrow[n \to \infty]{} f$ locally uniformly; and

II. $f$ is differentiable on $U$, and $f' = g$.

\textit{Note:} In this proof, we have used the following: if $X$ is a set, $Y$ is a complete metric space and $\{f_n\}_{n=1}^{\infty}$ is a sequence of maps defined on $X$ and taking values in $Y$, then $\{f_n\}_{n=1}^{\infty}$ is uniformly Cauchy if and only if it is uniformly convergent.

\section*{\textbf{Sufficient condition for differentiability:}}
Let $E_1, \ldots, E_n, F$ be real Banach spaces and $U \subseteq E_1 \times \cdots \times E_n$ be open. Suppose that $f : U \to F$ is such that $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_j}(x)$ exists for all $x \in E_1 \times \cdots \times E_n \setminus \{a\}$, where $a = (a_1, \ldots, a_n) \in U$, and $j = 1, \ldots, n - 1$, and $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}(a)$ exists. Assume that, for all $j = 1, \ldots, n - 1$, $T_j \in L(E_j; F)$ such that $\lim_{x \to a} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_j}(x) = T_j$. Then $f$ is differentiable at $a$, and $f'(a)h = \sum_{j=1}^{n-1} T_j h_j + \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}(a) h_n$, for all $h = (h_1, \ldots, h_n) \in E_1 \times \cdots \times E_n$.

\textbf{Remark 7:} In fact, in the above, if there exists $k \in \{1, \ldots, n\}$ such that $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_k}(a)$ exists, and for all $j \in \{1, \ldots, n\} \setminus \{k\}$, $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_j}(x)$ exists for all $x \in E_1 \times \cdots \times E_n \setminus \{a\}$ and $\lim_{x \to a} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_j}(x)$ exists in $L(E_j; F)$, say $T_j$, then $f$ is differentiable at $a$, and $f'(a)h = \sum_{1 \leq j \leq n, j \neq k} T_j h_j + \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_k}(a) h_k$, for all $h = (h_1, \ldots, h_n) \in E_1 \times \cdots \times E_n$.

\textbf{Corollary 3:} Let $E_1, \ldots, E_n, F$ be real Banach spaces, $U \subseteq E_1 \times \cdots \times E_n$ be open, and $f : U \to F$. Suppose that, for all $j = 1, \ldots, n$, $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_j}$ exists on $U \setminus \{a\}$, and there exists $T_j \in L(E_j; F)$ such that $\lim_{x \to a} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_j}(x) = T_j$. Then for all $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $\delta > 0$ such that

\[
\left\| f(x) - f(y) - \sum_{j=1}^{n} T_j (x_j - y_j) \right\| < \varepsilon \|x - y\|, \quad \forall x = (x_1, \ldots, x_n), y = (y_1, \ldots, y_n) \in B(a; \delta).
\]

Consequently, $f$ is differentiable at $a$. In particular, all partial derivatives $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_j}$ exist and continuous on $U$ if and only if $f \in C^1(U)$.

    \textbf{\textit{Inverse Mapping Theorem}}
\end{center}

\textbf{Inverse mapping theorem:} Let $E$ and $F$ are Banach spaces over $\mathbb{R}$, $a \in U \subseteq_{open} E$ and $f : U \to F$ be of class $C^1$. Assume that $f'(a)$ is invertible. Then there exist a $\Omega \subseteq_{open} U$ and $V \subseteq_{open} F$ such that $f|_{\Omega} : \Omega \to V$ bijective and $f|_{\Omega}^{-1} : V \to \Omega$ is of class $C^1$. Furthermore, for any $y \in V$, $(f|_{\Omega}^{-1})'(y) = (f'(f|_{\Omega}^{-1}(y)))^{-1}$.

\textbf{Remark 8:} As a part of the conclusion of the Inverse Mapping Theorem, one obtains that $f(a)$ is an interior point of $f(U)$. From this, it is immediate that, if $f'(x) \in \operatorname{Isom}(E; F)$, for all $x \in U$, then $f(U)$ is an open map, i.e., for any $W \subseteq_{open} U$, $f(W)$ is an open subset of $F$. In particular, if $U \subseteq_{open} \mathbb{C}$ and $f : U \to \mathbb{C}$ is holomorphic and $f'(z) \neq 0$, for all $z \in U$, then $f'(z)$ is nonsingular when viewed as a linear endomorphism of the real vector space $\mathbb{C}$ for all $z \in U$. Consequently, $f$ is an open map. This proves the "Open mapping theorem" of Complex analysis in one variable.

\textit{\textbf{Note:}} Let $E, F$ be Banach spaces over $K$, and $T \in L(E; F)$.

I. If $\delta := \inf_{\|x\|=1} \|Tx\| > 0$, then for all $S \in L(E; F)$ such that $\|S - T\| < \frac{\delta}{2}$, one has the following:

\[
\|Sx\| = \|Tx - (S - T)x\| \geq \|Tx\| - \|(S - T)x\| \geq \|Tx\| - \|T - S\|\|x\| \geq \frac{\delta}{2}\|x\|, \quad \forall x \in E.
\]

This in particular shows that $S$ is injective. From this, it follows that, if $E, F$ are finite dimensional, then the set of all injective linear maps from $E$ to $F$ is open in $L(E; F)$.

II. If there exists $A \in L(F; E)$ such that $T \circ A = I_F$, then any $S \in L(E; F)$ satisfying $\|S - T\| < \frac{1}{2\|A\|}$ is surjective. Indeed, given any $y \in F$ and $a \in E$, define the following sequence recursively:

\[
x_1 := a \text{ and } x_{n+1} := A(y + (T - S)x_n), \quad \forall n \in \mathbb{N}.
\tag{9}
\]

Then, using induction, one obtains that $\|x_{n+1} - x_n\| \leq \frac{1}{2^{n-1}} \|x_2 - x_1\|$. Clearly, $\{x_n\}_{n=1}^{\infty}$ is Cauchy since $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \|x_{n+1} - x_n\| < \infty$. Due to the completeness of $E$, there exists $x \in E$ such that $x_n \xrightarrow[n \to \infty]{} x$. Then from (9), we get $x = Ay + A(T - S)x$. We now apply $T$ to both sides to obtain $Sx = y$. When $E$ and $F$ are finite dimensional and $T : E \to F$ and $T \in L(E; F)$ is surjective, the existence of an $A \in L(F; E)$ such that $T \circ A = I_F$ is guaranteed. Hence, in view of the above, one concludes that the set of all surjective linear maps from $E$ to $F$ is open in $L(E; F)$, provided $E, F$ are finite dimensional.

\begin{center}
    \textbf{\textit{Implicit Mapping Theorem}}
\end{center}

\textbf{Implicit mapping theorem:} Let $E, F$ be Banach spaces over $\mathbb{R}$, $x_0 \in U \subseteq_{open} E$, $y_0 \in V \subseteq_{open} F$, and $f : U \times V \to F$ be a $C^1$ function such that $f(x_0, y_0) = 0$. Assume that $\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(x_0, y_0) \in \operatorname{Isom}(F; F)$. Then there exists $N(x_0) \subseteq_{open} U$, $N(y_0) \subseteq_{open} V$ and a $C^1$ map $\varphi : N(x_0) \to N(y_0)$ such that

\[
\forall (x, y) \in N(x_0) \times N(y_0), \quad f(x, y) = 0 \Longleftrightarrow y = \varphi(x).
\tag{10}
\]

Furthermore, for every $x \in N(x_0)$,
\[
\varphi'(x) = - \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(x, \varphi(x)) \right)^{-1} \circ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x, \varphi(x)).
\]

Note that (10) is equivalent to the following:

\[
f^{-1}(\{0\}) \cap (N(x_0) \times N(y_0)) = \{(x, \varphi(x)) : x \in N(x_0)\},
\]

i.e., the neighborhood $f^{-1}(\{0\}) \cap (N(x_0) \times N(y_0))$ of $(x_0, y_0)$ in $f^{-1}(\{0\})$ is the graph of the $C^1$ map $\varphi$.

\textbf{Remark 9:} It follows from (10) that, for any $x \in N(x_0)$, there exists a unique $y \in N(y_0)$ such that $f(x, y) = 0$. This establishes the uniqueness of the $C^1$ map $\varphi$ in a neighborhood of $x_0$. To be precise, if $x_0 \in \tilde{N}(x_0) \subseteq_{open} U$ and $y_0 \in \tilde{N}(y_0) \subseteq_{open} V$, and $\tilde{\varphi} : \tilde{N}(x_0) \to \tilde{N}(y_0)$ is a $C^1$ map such that for all $(x, y) \in \tilde{N}(x_0) \times \tilde{N}(y_0)$, the condition $f(x, y) = 0$ is equivalent to $y = \tilde{\varphi}(x)$, then $\varphi$ is equal to $\tilde{\varphi}$ on the intersection $N(x_0) \cap \tilde{N}(x_0)$.

The Inverse Mapping Theorem and the Implicit Mapping Theorem are equivalent. Indeed, the latter can be deduced from the former as follows. Consider the map $F : U \times V \to E \times F$, $F(x, y) := (x, f(x, y))$. Then, for all $(x, y) \in U \times V$, one has

\[
F'(x, y)(h, k) = \left( h, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x, y)h + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(x, y)k \right), \quad \text{for all } (h, k) \in E \times F.
\]

In particular,

\[
F'(x_0, y_0)(h, k) = \left( h, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x_0, y_0)h + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(x_0, y_0)k \right), \quad \text{for all } (h, k) \in E \times F.
\tag{11}
\]

Note that $F'$ is continuous in view of Exercise 7, and the inverse of the continuous linear map mentioned above in (11) is given by the following:

\[
E \times F \to E \times F, \; (\xi, \zeta) \mapsto \left( \xi, - \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(x_0, y_0) \right)^{-1} \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x_0, y_0)\xi - \zeta \right) \right).
\]

Now the Inverse mapping theorem yields $r > 0$ and $(x_0, y_0) \in W \subseteq_{open} U \times V$ such that $F : W \to B((x_0, 0); r)$ and $F^{-1} : B((x_0, 0); r) \to W$ both are $C^1$. Refining the choice of $r > 0$, if necessary, we get $B(x_0; r) \subseteq U$. Denote by $q$ the projection map $q : E \times F \to F$ onto $F$. Clearly $q(W)$ is open in $F$. Let

\[
\varphi : B(x_0; r) \to q(W), \quad \varphi(x) := q(F^{-1}(x, 0)).
\]

It can be readily seen that $\varphi$ is of class $C^1$, and for $(x, y) \in B(x_0; r) \times q(W)$,

\[
f(x, y) = 0 \Longleftrightarrow F(x, y) = (x, 0) \Longleftrightarrow (x, y) = F^{-1}(x, 0) \Longleftrightarrow q(F^{-1}(x, 0)) = y.
\]

Provide an example that $T_p S \subsetneq \ker g'(p)$, when $p$ is not a regular point.

Denote the coordinates of $g$ by $g_1, \ldots, g_m$. Since, the $i$-th row of the Jacobian $\frac{\partial (g_1,\ldots,g_m)}{\partial (x_1,\ldots,x_{n+m})}(p)$ is $\nabla g_i(p)$, for each $i = 1, \ldots, m$, it is easy to see that

\[
v \in \ker g'(p) \Longleftrightarrow v \in (\operatorname{span}_{\mathbb{R}}\{\nabla g_1(p), \ldots, \nabla g_m(p)\})^\perp.
\]

Thus one has $T_p S = (\operatorname{span}_{\mathbb{R}}\{\nabla g_1(p), \ldots, \nabla g_m(p)\})^\perp$, which is equivalent to the following:

\[
(T_p S)^\perp = \operatorname{span}_{\mathbb{R}}\{\nabla g_1(p), \ldots, \nabla g_m(p)\}.
\tag{12}
\]

Suppose that $f : U \to \mathbb{R}$ is a differentiable function such that $p$ is a point of local extremum of $f|_S$. Then, for any $v \in T_p S$, it is readily seen that

\[
\nabla f(p) \cdot v = f'(p)v = \frac{d}{dt}\Big|_{t=0} f(\gamma(t)) = 0,
\]

where $\varepsilon > 0$, $\gamma : (-\varepsilon, \varepsilon) \to \mathbb{R}^{n+m}$ is a $C^1$ map such that $\gamma(-\varepsilon, \varepsilon) \subseteq S$, $\gamma(0) = p$ and $\gamma'(0) = v$. This shows that $\nabla f(p) \in (T_p S)^\perp$. In view of (12), it is now immediate that there exist $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_m \in \mathbb{R}$ such that

\[
\nabla f(p) = \lambda_1 \nabla g_1(p) + \cdots + \lambda_m \nabla g_m(p).
\tag{13}
\]

The numbers $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_m$ are called the \textbf{\textit{Lagrange's multipliers}}


\textbf{Remark 10:}

I. Lagrange's multiplier method provides a necessary condition for a constrained local extremum. Hence, assuming all points of $S$ are regular, the points obtained by solving (13) must be tested further to determine whether they yield a local extremum. Indeed, there may exists $p \in S$ for which (13) may hold for some $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_m \in \mathbb{R}$, however, $p$ is not a point of local extremum. To see this, consider the example $g : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$, $g(x, y) := y$, $f : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$, $f(x, y) := x^3$, and $S := g^{-1}(\{0\}) = \{(x, 0) : x \in \mathbb{R}\}$. It is immediate that, although $\nabla f(0, 0) = 0 = 0 \cdot \nabla g(0, 0)$, $f|_S$ does not have a local maximum or minimum at $(0, 0)$.

II. Let $h : \mathbb{R}^{n+1} \times \mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}$, $h(x, y) := f(x) - y \cdot g(x)$. It is easy to see that, if (13) holds for $p \in S$ and $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_m \in \mathbb{R}$, then $\nabla h(p, \lambda) = 0$, where $\lambda := (\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_m)$. This shows that adding Lagrange multipliers converts a constrained problem into an unconstrained one, and the necessary condition (13) for constrained local extremum is recovered from the first order necessary condition for unconstrained local extremum applied to $h$.

\textbf{Spectral theorem for symmetric matrices:} Let $n \in \mathbb{N}$ and $A \in M_n(\mathbb{R})$ is symmetric. Then there exists $P \in O(n)$ such that $P^{-1} A P$ is diagonal.

To see this, consider the function $g_1(x) = \|x\|^2 - 1$, $\forall x \in \mathbb{R}^n$. Then $S := g_1^{-1}(0)$ is the unit sphere $S^{n-1}$. Let $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$, $f(x) := Ax \cdot x = x^t A x$. Note that, for all $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$, $\nabla f(x) = 2Ax$ and $\nabla g_1(x) = 2x$. Since $S^{n-1}$ is compact, there exists $v_1 \in S^{n-1}$ such that $f(v_1) = \max_{\|x\|=1} f(x)$. From (13), it follows that, there exists $\lambda_1 \in \mathbb{R}$ such that $\nabla f(v_1) = \lambda_1 \nabla g_1(v_1)$, i.e., $Av_1 = \lambda_1 v_1$. We now introduce $g_2 : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$, $g_2(x) := x \cdot v_1$. Then $\nabla g_2(x) = v_1$, for all $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$. Again, since $\{x \in \mathbb{R}^n : g_1(x) = g_2(x) = 0\} = \{x \in S^{n-1} : x \cdot v_1 = 0\}$ is compact, in view of (13), we obtain an unit vector $v_2$ orthogonal to $v_1$ and $\lambda_2, \sigma \in \mathbb{R}$ such that $Av_2 = \lambda_2 v_2 + \sigma v_1$. Clearly

\[
\sigma = \sigma v_1 \cdot v_1 = Av_2 \cdot v_1 = v_2 \cdot Av_1 = \lambda_1 v_2 \cdot v_1 = 0,
\]

since $v_1$ is an eigenvector with eigenvalue $\lambda_1$.

Thus $\lambda_2$ is an eigenvalue. Note that $\lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2$ and $v_1, v_2$ are orthogonal to each other. Continuing this process, we obtain eigenvalues $\lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_{n-1}$ and an orthogonal set of vectors $\{v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_{n-1}\} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ such that $Av_i = \lambda_i v_i$, for all $i = 1, \ldots, n-1$. There exists a unit vector $v_n \in \{v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_{n-1}\}^\perp$. It is clear that $\{v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_n\}$ is an orthonormal basis of $\mathbb{R}^n$. Hence, $Av_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n-1} \sigma_i v_i + \lambda_n v_n$, for some $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_{n-1}, \lambda_n \in \mathbb{R}$. Taking the dot product of both sides with $v_1, \ldots, v_{n-1}$, one obtains that $\sigma_1 = \cdots = \sigma_{n-1} = 0$, $A$ is symmetric. This shows that $v_n$ is an eigen vector corresponding to the eigenvalue $\lambda_n$. Also note that, $\lambda_{n-1} \geq \lambda_n$. Finally, consider the $n \times n$ matrix $P$ such that the $i$-th column of $P$ is $v_i$, for all $i = 1, \ldots, n$.

\varphi(t) = \varphi(0) + \varphi'(0)t + \varphi''(0)\frac{t^2}{2} + R(t), \text{ where } \frac{R(t)}{t^2} \to 0.
\tag{19}
\]

Suppose now that $f$ has a local minimum at $a$, $r > 0$ is such that $B(a; r) \subseteq U$ and $f(x) \geq f(a)$, for all $x \in B(a; r)$, and $h \in B(0; r)$. Then (18) and (19) yield

\[
0 \leq f(a + th) - f(a) = \varphi(t) - \varphi(0) = \varphi'(0)t + \varphi''(0)\frac{t^2}{2} + R(t) = f''(a)(h, h)\frac{t^2}{2} + R(t), \ \forall t \in (-\varepsilon, 1 + \varepsilon).
\]

This shows that $f''(a)(h, h) + \frac{R(t)}{t^2} \geq 0$, for all $t \in (-\varepsilon, 1 + \varepsilon) \setminus \{0\}$. As a result, $f''(a)(h, h) = \lim_{t \to 0} \left( f''(a)(h, h) + \frac{R(t)}{t^2} \right) \geq 0$, which further implies that $f''(a)(v, v) \geq 0$, holds for $v \in E$, i.e., the symmetric bilinear form $f''(a)$ is positive. Thus, we deduce the following necessary criterion for $f$ to have a local minimum at $a$:

\[
f \text{ is twice differentiable at } a \text{ and it has a local minimum at } a, \text{ then } f''(a) \text{ is positive}.
\tag{20}
\]

Similar to (20), one can prove the necessary condition for $a$ to be a point of local maximum of $f$, when $f$ is twice differentiable at $a$.

Let $n,k \in \mathbb{N}$, $E_1,\dots,E_n,F$ be Banach spaces over $\mathbb{R}$,

Let $E,F$ be real Banach spaces, $U \subseteq E$ open and $f : U \to F$.
If $f$ is $k$ times differentiable at a point $a \in U$, where $k \in \mathbb{N}$,
it follows from (21) that the derivative of the map
\[
E \longrightarrow F, \qquad h \longmapsto f^{(k)}(a)(h,\dots,h)
\]
at any $h \in E$ is the following continuous linear map
\[
E \longrightarrow F, \qquad \xi \longmapsto k\, f^{(k)}(a)(h,\dots,h,\xi).
\]

Since $f^{(k)}(a) = (f')^{(k-1)}(a)$, we may treat
\[
f^{(k)}(a)(h,\dots,h,\xi)
\]
as
\[
(f')^{(k-1)}(a)(h,\dots,h)(\xi),
\]
for all $h,\xi \in E$, owing to the identification
\[
L(\underbrace{E,\dots,E}_{k\ \text{copies}};F)
\;\cong\;
L(\underbrace{E,\dots,E}_{k-1\ \text{copies}};L(E;F)).
\]

Thus the derivative of
\[
E \longrightarrow F, \qquad h \longmapsto f^{(k)}(a)(h,\dots,h)
\]
is simply
\[
E \longrightarrow L(E;F), \qquad
h \longmapsto k\, (f')^{(k-1)}(a)(h,\dots,h).
\]

Choose $r>0$ such that $B(a;r) \subseteq U$.
Assume now that $f$ is $n$ times differentiable at $a$, where $n \in \mathbb{N}$.
Consider the function
\[
\varphi : B(a;r) \longrightarrow F,
\qquad
\varphi(h)
:=
f(a+h)
-
\sum_{k=0}^{n}
\frac{1}{k!}\, f^{(k)}(a)(h,\dots,h).
\]

Then it follows that
\[
\varphi'(h)
=
f'(a+h)
-
\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}
\frac{1}{k!}\,(f')^{(k)}(a)(h,\dots,h)
\in L(E;F),
\qquad
\text{whenever } \|h\|<r.
\tag{24}
\]

In view of (24), using induction and the Mean Value Theorem,
one proves the corresponding asymptotic property.

\begin{center}
    \textbf{\textit{Taylor's Formula}}
\end{center}

\textbf{Taylor's formula:}
Let $E,F$ be Banach spaces over $\mathbb{R}$,
$U \subseteq_{\mathrm{open}} E$, and $f : U \to F$ be $n$ times differentiable at $a \in U$.
Then
\[
f(a+h)
=
f(a)
+
\sum_{k=1}^{n}
\frac{1}{k!}\, f^{(k)}(a)h^k
+
o(\|h\|^n),
\tag{25}
\]
where for each $k=1,\dots,n$, $h^k$ denotes the multivector $(h,\dots,h) \in E^k$.

\bigskip

\textbf{Remark 11:}

\begin{enumerate}
\item
\[
f(a)
+
\sum_{k=1}^{n}
\frac{1}{k!}\, f^{(k)}(a)(x-a)^k
\]
is often referred to as the $n$-th order best approximation of $f$ at $a$.

\item
Let $E=\mathbb{R}^d$, where $d \in \mathbb{N}$.
Given $1 \le k \le n$ and $i_1,\dots,i_d \in \{0,1,\dots,k\}$ such that
\[
i_1+\cdots+i_d = k,
\]
there are exactly
\[
\frac{k!}{i_1!\cdots i_d!}
\]
$d$-tuples in which each $j=1,\dots,d$ occurs $i_j$ times.
Hence, in this case (25) can be rewritten as
\[
f(a+h)
=
f(a)
+
\sum_{k=1}^{n}
\left(
\sum_{i_1+\cdots+i_d=k}
\frac{h_1^{\,i_1}\cdots h_d^{\,i_d}}{i_1!\cdots i_d!}
\,
\frac{\partial^k f}{\partial x_1^{i_1}\cdots\partial x_d^{i_d}}(a)
\right)
+
o(\|h\|^n).
\]
\end{enumerate}

\bigskip

An important application of Taylor's formula is the following sufficient condition for a local minimum.

\medskip

\textbf{\textit{Second-derivative test for a local minimum:}}

Let $E$ be a real Banach space and $f : U \to \mathbb{R}$ be twice differentiable at $a \in U$.
Assume that
\[
f'(a)=0
\quad \text{and} \quad
f''(a)
\text{ is positive and non-degenerate}.
\]
Then $a$ is a point of local minimum of $f$.

\medskip

\textbf{Note:}
The following crucial property of positive non-degenerate bilinear forms
has been used in the proof above:
\[
\langle \cdot , \cdot \rangle \in L(E,E;\mathbb{R})
\text{ positive and non-degenerate }
\;\Longrightarrow\;
\exists \lambda>0 \text{ such that }
\langle x,x\rangle \ge \lambda \|x\|^2,
\quad \forall x \in E.
\tag{26}
\]

When $E$ is finite dimensional, (26) is an immediate consequence
of the compactness of the sphere
\[
\{x \in E : \|x\|=1\}.
\]

\textbf{Remark 12:}

\begin{enumerate}
\item
If $E=\mathbb{R}^n$, where $n \in \mathbb{N}$, then $f''(a)$ is positive and
non-degenerate if and only if all eigenvalues of the Hessian matrix
\[
\nabla^2 f(a)
=
\left(
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_i \partial x_j}(a)
\right)_{i,j=1,\dots,n}
\]
are positive. This condition is further equivalent to requiring that all
principal minors
\[
\left(
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_i \partial x_j}(a)
\right)_{i,j=1,\dots,k}
\]
have positive determinant. Considering $-f$, one obtains a similar sufficient
condition for a local maximum of $f$.

\item
If $f'(a)=0$ but $f''(a)$ is neither positive nor negative, then $a$
cannot be a point of local extremum of $f$. Such a point $a$ is said to be
a \emph{saddle point} of $f$. Note that, if $a \in U$ is a saddle point of $f$,
then for every $r>0$ there exist $x,y \in B(a;r)$ such that
\[
f(x) < f(a) < f(y).
\]

\item
If $a \in U$ is such that $f'(a)=0$, then we say that $a$ is a
\emph{critical point} of $f$. If, in addition, $f$ is twice differentiable
and $f''(a)$ is non-degenerate, then $a$ is called a
\emph{non-degenerate critical point} of $f$; otherwise $a$ is called a
\emph{degenerate critical point}. A degenerate critical point may be a
point of local extremum or a saddle point. Consider the following examples:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $f : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$,
\[
f(x,y) := x^2 + y^3.
\]
\item $f : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$,
\[
f(x,y) := x^2 + y^4.
\]
\end{enumerate}

In both examples we have
\[
\nabla f(0,0)=0
\quad \text{and} \quad
\nabla^2 f(0,0)
=
\begin{pmatrix}
2 & 0 \\
0 & 0
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
However, in the former case $(0,0)$ is a saddle point, while in the latter
case it is a point of absolute minimum.
\end{enumerate}

\bigskip

\textbf{Corollary 4:}

Let $U \subseteq_{\mathrm{open}} \mathbb{R}^2$ and
$f : U \to \mathbb{R}$ be twice differentiable at $a \in U$.
Denote by $\Delta$ the determinant of the Hessian matrix of $f$ at $a$.
Then:

\begin{enumerate}
\item
\[
\Delta > 0
\quad \text{and} \quad
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1^2}(a) > 0
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
a \text{ is a point of local minimum of } f.
\]

\item
\[
\Delta > 0
\quad \text{and} \quad
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1^2}(a) < 0
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
a \text{ is a point of local maximum of } f.
\]

\item
\[
\Delta < 0
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
a \text{ is a saddle point of } f.
\]
\end{enumerate}

2. Let $A \in L(E; F)$. Consider $f : U \to F$, $f(x) := Ax$. Then $f \in C^{\infty}(U)$. In fact, $f^{(n)} = 0$, for all $n \geq 2$.

This gives rise to a map $B'$ from $E_1 \times E_2$ to $L(E_1 \times E_2; F)$ as follows:

\[
B' : E_1 \times E_2 \to L(E_1 \times E_2; F), \quad (x_1, x_2) \mapsto B'(x_1, x_2).
\]

One can easily verify that $B'$ is continuous and linear. Consider $f : U \to F$, $f(x_1, x_2) := B(x_1, x_2)$. Then $f \in C^{\infty}(U)$. In fact, $f'(x_1, x_2) = B'(x_1, x_2)$, $f''(x_1, x_2) = B'$, for all $(x_1, x_2) \in U$. Consequently, $f^{(n)} = 0$, for all $n \geq 3$.

Observe that $\forall A \in \operatorname{Isom}(E; F)$, $\varphi'(A) = -\psi(\varphi(A), \varphi(A))$. Hence, by a routine induction argument, one obtains that $\varphi$ is of class $C^{\infty}$. Note that, in the special case $E = F = K^n$, where $n \in \mathbb{N}$, $\varphi \in C^{\infty}(GL_n(K))$ follows at once from the observation that each entry of $A^{-1}$ is a rational function defined on $GL_n(K)$ in the entries of $A$ (use Part III and VI of Exercise 31).

\textbf{Remark 13:} An important implication of the last example is that if we assume the map $f$ to be of class $C^n$ in the hypothesis of the Inverse Mapping Theorem, where $n \in \mathbb{N}$, then the inverse map $f|_{\Omega}^{-1}$ belongs to $C^n(V)$. This can be easily proven by induction. From this, we can also conclude that the map $\varphi$ derived from the Implicit Mapping Theorem is of class $C^n$, provided that $f \in C^n(U \times V)$. Similarly, the diffeomorphisms $\varphi$ and $\psi$ mentioned in the conclusion of the Rank theorem are of class $C^n$ if $f \in C^n(U)$.

The key idea behind this construction is that for given $0 < a < b$, there is a $C^{\infty}$ function defined on $\mathbb{R}$ which vanishes identically outside $[a, b]$. The following is an example of such a function:

\[

\begin{cases}
e^{\left( \frac{1}{x-a} - \frac{1}{x-b} \right)}, & \text{if } a < x < b; \\

\end{cases}

From this, it is easy to construct a $h \in C^{\infty}(\mathbb{R})$ such that

\textbf{Corollary 5:} Let $n \in \mathbb{N}$, $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ be open, and $f : U \to \mathbb{R}$ be smooth. Then, for every $a \in U$, there exists an open neighborhood $V \subseteq U$ of $a$ and $g \in C^{\infty}(\mathbb{R}^n)$ such that $f = g$ on $V$. In fact, $g$ can be chosen so that $g \equiv 0$ on $\mathbb{R}^n \setminus U$.

Let $[a, b] \subseteq \mathbb{R}$, where $a < b$ be a compact interval, $E, F$ be Banach spaces over $\mathbb{R}$, $U$ be an open subset of $E$, and $\varphi : [a, b] \times U \to F$ be continuous. A standard compactness argument shows the following:

\[
\forall x \in U \text{ and } \varepsilon > 0 \; \exists \delta > 0 \text{ such that } \|y - x\| < \delta \Longrightarrow \|\varphi(t, x) - \varphi(t, y)\| < \varepsilon, \text{ for all } t \in [a, b].
\tag{29}
\]

\begin{center}
    \textbf{\textit{Leibniz's Rule}}
\end{center}

\textbf{Leibniz's rule:} Let $E$ be a real Banach space, $U \subseteq E$ be open, $a < b$, and $f : [a, b] \times U \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuous. Consider

\[
F : U \to \mathbb{R}, \quad F(x) := \int_a^b f(t, x)\, dt.
\tag{30}
\]

Then $F$ is continuous. If, in addition, $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} : [a, b] \times U \to L(E; \mathbb{R})$ is continuous, then $F$ is continuously differentiable on $U$, and

\[
\forall x \in U, \quad F'(x)h = \int_a^b \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(t, x)h \, dt, \text{ for all } h \in E.
\tag{31}
\]

\textit{Note:} Since $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} : [a, b] \times U \to L(E; \mathbb{R})$ is continuous, it satisfies (29). This helps us to estimate the following integral:

\[
\int_a^b \left\| f(t, x + h) - f(t, x) - \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(t, x)h \right\| dt,
\]

once we apply the Mean Value Theorem to the integrand. The continuity of $F'$ also follows from (29) for $\varphi = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}$.


\textbf{Remark 14:} In the special case $E = \mathbb{R}^n$, where $n \in \mathbb{N}$, (31) becomes

\[
\forall x \in U, \quad F'(x)h = \sum_{j=1}^{n} h_j \int_a^b \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_j}(t, x)\, dt, \text{ for all } h \in \mathbb{R}^n.
\]

In particular, if $n = 1$, we have

\[
\forall x \in U, \quad F'(x) = \int_a^b \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(t, x)\, dt.
\]

An interesting application of Leibniz's rule is the evaluation of the Gaussian integral $\int_0^{\infty} e^{-x^2} dx$. Using the comparison test, it is easy to see that this improper integral exists. We consider the functions $f, g : [0, \infty) \to \mathbb{R}$ defined as follows:

\[
f(x) := \left( \int_0^x e^{-t^2} dt \right)^2 \quad \text{and} \quad g(x) := \int_0^1 \frac{e^{-x^2(1+t^2)}}{1+t^2} dt.
\]

Then, for all $x \in \mathbb{R}$, one has $f'(x) = 2e^{-x^2} \int_0^x e^{-t^2} dt$, and

\[
g'(x) = -2e^{-x^2} \int_0^1 e^{-(xt)^2} dt = -2e^{-x^2} \int_0^x e^{-t^2} dt,
\]

by Leibniz's rule. This shows that $f' + g' \equiv 0$, consequently, $f + g \equiv f(0) + g(0) = \frac{\pi}{4}$. Now since

\[
0 \leq g(x) = \int_0^1 \frac{e^{-x^2(1+t^2)}}{1+t^2} dt \leq \left( \int_0^1 \frac{1}{1+t^2} dt \right) e^{-x^2} \xrightarrow[x \to \infty]{} 0,
\]

it follows that

\[
\int_0^{\infty} e^{-x^2} dx = \lim_{x \to \infty} \int_0^x e^{-t^2} dt = \lim_{x \to \infty} \sqrt{f(x)} = \sqrt{\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x)} = \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2}.
\]
